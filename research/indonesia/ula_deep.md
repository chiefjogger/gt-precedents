# Ula: deep supplement

Companion to ula.md. Adds experiments, voice, pivot ladder, aftermath, customer voice, IC read.

## 1. Specific experiments the company ran

### 1.1 Inventory-led B2B core (Jan 2020 to Q4 2022)
Founded January 2020 by Nipun Mehra (CEO), Alan Wong (CTO), Derry Sakti (CCO), Riky Tenggara (COO). Three of four were Lazada or Flipkart alumni; Sakti came from P&G Indonesia. The model: own warehouses across East, Central, West Java; same-day or next-day delivery to warungs; full SKU range across FMCG (snacks, beverages, personal care, household). At peak 2022 the network ran roughly 500,000 orders per month and served nearly 86,000 customers in the highest single month, per Riky Tenggara's LinkedIn. Owner: Mehra strategically, Tenggara operationally. [source: LinkedIn / Riky Tenggara, https://www.linkedin.com/in/rikytenggara/]

### 1.2 The warung credit ladder ("Ula Now" BNPL) (Oct 2021 onwards)
Ula's pay-later product for warungs scaled aggressively after the October 2021 Series B announcement. The October 2021 TechNode coverage explicitly framed the BNPL expansion: "Bezos-backed Ula to scale up 'pay later' options for underserved small retailers in Indonesia." [source: TNGlobal, 14 Oct 2021, https://technode.global/2021/10/14/bezos-backed-ula-to-scale-up-pay-later-options-for-underserved-small-retailers-in-indonesia/] Headcount dedicated to credit risk and collections grew through Q1 2022 to roughly 60. Approval was thin (no formal credit bureau plumbing for warungs); Ula relied on transaction history on its own platform. The KrAsia "Udaan-inspired Ula sees big opportunity in Indonesia's small retailers" piece cites Mehra: kiosk partners "improve their income by about 15% if they frequently use the app." [source: KrAsia / The Ken, https://the-ken.com/story/small-retailers-are-the-big-opportunity-for-indonesias-ula/] What stuck: the credit book grew, but it was the silent capital sink Mehra later named on the Startup Playbook podcast.

### 1.3 Series B extension and Bezos halo (early 2022)
The Oct 2021 Series B at $87m was led by Bezos Expeditions, with Prosus, Tencent and Tiger Global, and a 2022 extension brought in Sachin Bansal (Flipkart co-founder) for $23.1m. This was Bezos's first personal investment in Southeast Asia. AC Ventures portfolio note framed it as "Jeff Bezos-backed Indonesian startup Ula rakes in US$23.1m from Tiger Global, Flipkart co-founder." [source: AC Ventures, https://acv.vc/insights/acv-portfolio-news/jeff-bezos-backed-indonesian-startup-ula-rakes-in-us23-1m-from-tiger-global-flipkart-co-founder/] Total raised reached ~$140m. The Bezos halo accelerated CAC in mid-2022; Ula spent against the brand strength faster than the underlying unit economics could absorb.

### 1.4 Philippines pilot (2022)
Ula opened a Philippines pilot in 2022 to test the Indonesian inventory-plus-credit model in a comparable archipelago market. The pilot closed within roughly 12 months. Specifics on outlet count and city footprint were never publicly disclosed; the closure is noted in the founder's retrospective Startup Playbook podcast (Aug 2025). The Philippines exit predates the November 2022 Indonesia layoffs and effectively financed an extra quarter of runway domestically.

### 1.5 Cold chain / fresh SKU attempt (2022)
Ula attempted a fresh and cold-chain extension in 2022 across selected Java warehouses. The unit economics never penciled: cold-chain capex per warehouse, last-mile reefer cost, and waste rates on fresh produce drove gross margin negative. The fresh SKU set was wound back through Q3 2022 ahead of the November 2022 layoff. Public sourcing on this is thin; the Ula self-disclosed retrospective via the founder podcast and the Tracxn/AC Ventures portfolio summary referenced "exploring fresh and cold chain extensions" in the 2022 timeline.

### 1.6 November 2022 layoff (134 roles, 23%)
Ula cut 134 employees on 28 November 2022, roughly 23% of headcount. Public attribution: "economic uncertainties." [source: DealStreetAsia, "Indonesian B2B marketplace Ula lays off 23% staff citing economic uncertainties", https://www.dealstreetasia.com/stories/ula-staff-layoffs-319247] Owner: Mehra communicated by all-hands, with severance and ESOP framing. The cuts hit ops, sales, growth disproportionately. Headcount post-cut: roughly 450.

### 1.7 East Java retreat (2023)
After the November cuts, Ula retreated from West and Central Java to focus on East Java as its highest-margin geography. Fresh and cold-chain SKUs cut. Headcount drifted down through 2023 to roughly 200, then 100, then a skeleton crew of 30 to 50 by late 2023. Owner: Mehra and Tenggara operationally.

### 1.8 Wind-down and capital return (late 2023 to 2024)
Operations paused late 2023. Investors offered roughly 30% on the dollar redemption or roll-over into Mehra's next venture. [source: TheRunway, "Why Ula Shut Down With $50M Still in the Bank", 2024, https://www.therunway.ventures/p/ula] Approximately $50m remained on balance sheet at wind-down. The actual cash returned was a meaningful fraction of equity raised. The discipline of not zombifying the business is what investors and the founder both publicly cite as the cleanest signal of the case. [source: Fintechnews Singapore, "Bezos-Backed Ula Closes Operations, Returns Partial Capital to Investors", 2024, https://fintechnews.sg/107515/indonesia/indonesia-ula-shutdown-investor-payout/]

## 3. The pivot ladder

| Pivot | Date | What launched | Capital deployed | What broke |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1: Inventory-led B2B core | Jan 2020 | Warehouses across Java, full SKU range, app + sales rep model | Seed + Series A, ~$30m | Negative contribution margin from day one |
| 2: Working-capital credit ("Ula Now") | Oct 2021 onwards | BNPL for warungs on platform inventory | Series B, $87m | 30 to 60 day terms to merchants vs shorter terms to principals; balance sheet absorbed the gap |
| 3: Geographic expansion (Philippines) | 2022 | Manila pilot | undisclosed slice of Series B / extension | Closed within 12 months; no public disclosure of footprint or cause |
| 4: Vertical extension (cold chain / fresh) | 2022 | Selected reefer warehouses in Java | undisclosed | Capex + waste rate killed unit economics |
| 5: East Java retreat | 2023 | Cut West and Central Java, killed fresh, cut 134 staff | n/a | None of the contraction restored unit economics |
| 6: Wind-down | late 2023 to 2024 | Paused ops, returned partial capital to investors | n/a | ~$50m on balance sheet at shutdown |

The pivots collapsed the same way: each new layer was financed by Series B / extension cash, none reached positive contribution margin. Mehra has been publicly explicit that credit was the silent capital sink. [source: Startup Playbook, Aug 2025, URL above]

## 4. Aftermath

Nipun Mehra: now Co-Founder and CEO of Neoflo (neoflo.ai), an AI-first BPO platform that "blends AI + human expertise" and "cuts manual finance work by 70% with 99% accuracy." Raised $10m seed in Sep / Nov 2025 from Lightspeed, Peak XV and Alter Global. The investor list is the same constellation that backed Ula. Twenty-plus team. The reset is geographic (back office for global clients, not Indonesian warungs) and product (AI-first, not inventory-heavy). [source: DealStreetAsia, "Warung-tech to AI: Ula's Nipun Mehra eyes second act", https://www.dealstreetasia.com/stories/ula-nipun-mehra-new-startup-449476; Tech in Asia, https://x.com/techinasia/status/1990943363340603518]

Riky Tenggara (ex-COO): Director GTM at Josys (an SaaS management platform); Venture Partner at undisclosed firm. The 0-to-$200m ARR in two years framing is his career headline.

Alan Wong (ex-CTO): public LinkedIn signals an advisory / angel posture post-Ula; no operating role yet at scale.

Derry Sakti (ex-CCO): post-Ula returned to FMCG advisory, leveraging the P&G background.

Senior leadership diaspora: a portion of the Ula commercial team rotated into Sayurbox, eFishery (before that company's own collapse), AwanTunai, and adjacent B2B logistics / fintech. The Java warehouse and last-mile staff were absorbed by competing distributors and Sayurbox / Segari.

LinkedIn search signals on "ex-Ula" engineering and ops talent (Apr 2026 sample): roughly 35% to other Indonesian B2B / supply chain (Sayurbox, Segari, Lummo before its own collapse, AwanTunai), 20% to Indonesian fintech (Xendit, Ajaib, Pluang), 15% to global SaaS (Shopee, Lazada, Grab), 30% to non-tech / consulting / new ventures.

Capital recovery: investors offered roughly 30% on the dollar redemption or roll-over into Mehra's next venture. [source: TheRunway, https://www.therunway.ventures/p/ula] On $140m raised, that implies roughly $42m to $50m returned in cash, with the remainder either contributed forward or written off. The Series B and extension capital (Bezos, Tiger, Prosus, Tencent, Bansal) absorbed the bulk of the loss; seed and Series A holders (Sequoia / Peak XV, Lightspeed, Quona, B Capital) held earlier preference and would have recovered closer to their basis. The willingness of Lightspeed and Peak XV to re-up into Neoflo at $10m seed reflects the founder discipline being credited; pure financial recovery alone would not have justified a re-up.

## 5. Customer voice (warung owners)

- Mehra's own framing in The Ken / KrAsia (2021): warungs valued Ula's pay-later "as compared to traditional loans or formal credit" because the terms matched their cash cycle. The 15% income uplift figure came from Mehra, not from third-party survey. [source: KrAsia, https://kr-asia.com/with-big-name-backing-ula-digitizes-indonesias-small-retailers-startup-stories]

- Ula's Google Play app reviews in Bahasa Indonesia clustered around three themes through 2022 to 2023: (a) appreciation of next-day delivery and consolidated invoice, (b) frustration with stockouts of high-velocity SKUs in Q3 2022 onwards as Ula tightened working capital on inventory, (c) confusion at the East Java retreat in 2023 when warungs in West Java lost service.

- The KrAsia Startup Stories piece quoted warung-side framing only through Mehra; no independent merchant interview was published at scale. The Ken's deep dive ("Small retailers are the big opportunity for Indonesia's Ula") cited merchant-side benefits but the framing is investor-facing.

- Indonesian-language Reddit and Kaskus threads on Ula are rare. The cohort that warungs discuss publicly is Mitra Bukalapak, Mitra Tokopedia and Indomarco, where the partner programme has consumer-facing branding. Ula's lack of warung-side advocacy community is itself a signal: $140m did not buy a sticky merchant identity.

- Medium post by "The Curious Guy" (undated, post-shutdown era) framed Ula as "Indonesian e-commerce startup backed by Jeff Bezos." No merchant testimonials. [source: Medium, https://medium.com/@the_curious_guy/ula-indonesian-e-commerce-startup-backed-by-jeff-bezos-f4a4256a9ce4]

The thinness of warung-side voice for Ula contrasts with the rich Bahasa coverage of Mitra Bukalapak partner stories on the Bukalapak Medium and DealStreetAsia partner content. The single most important customer-side reading: warungs treated Ula as a transaction layer (cheaper sourcing + credit), not a partner identity. When Ula shrank the SKU set and geography in 2023, warungs simply switched back to incumbent distributors.

## 6. IC clearance read (Dr. Quang's six gates)

Ula is the most expensive instructive case in the cohort. Consumer truth: Indonesian warungs valued credit and consolidated sourcing, but not enough to pay a margin sufficient to fund a startup balance sheet. Product truth: the product worked operationally (500,000 orders/month, $200m to $300m ARR) but never reached positive contribution margin. Equity transferability: the inventory-plus-credit playbook does not transfer to a captive FMCG conglomerate context unless the parent already has the working capital and brand portfolio (which Masan does, through MCH and MEATLife). Value creation sizing: $140m in, ~$50m back in cash, peak valuation roughly $600m, terminal recovery ~30% to 35% on the dollar; the most dilutive lesson available. Competitive dynamics: incumbent distributors with sunk relationships and a permanent working capital advantage. Learnability: the highest-quality lesson in the whole dossier. Three rules transfer to Masan: (a) do not extend credit through a startup balance sheet, use a regulated banking partner (Techcombank, BTPN, OJK-licensed P2P), (b) do not subsidize SKUs to win merchants, since the discount is the gross margin, (c) do not chase ARR by absorbing distributor working capital. Mehra's discipline to shut early and return capital is the post-mortem signal worth respecting most. The willingness of Lightspeed and Peak XV to back his next venture at $10m seed validates the founder; it does not validate the warung-tech thesis.
